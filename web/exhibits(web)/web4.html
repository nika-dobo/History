From the London Whirlpool Factory

🌀 Exhibition: “The Rotating Disc of Emotion” — From the London Whirlpool Factory 📍 Location: Hall of Britain | Alternative Technologies Section (1800–1900) ⚙️ Object: A complex disc mechanism constructed of copper, glass, and steel 🕰️ History: In 1864, in an alternative London, in the midst of the Industrial Revolution, engineer and weird science enthusiast Christopher Simonett created a new type of factory — the “Emotion Processing Machine,” whose goal was to translate people’s inexplicable feelings into mechanical language. The centerpiece of this factory was the “rotating disc” — a rotating device that, when touched, recorded a person’s psychological state and conveyed their emotions through rotating symbols and colors. 💠 How the disc worked: The user placed his hand on a special glass surface The mechanism began to rotate — the copper spirals inside captured the body's microelectric impulses Based on this, the disc displayed one of the emotional colors and symbols (for example, for fear — a black circle, for hope — a blue sun, for resentment — a yellow tear) A special institution was opened in London — the “Vortex Factory,” where people went to meet their emotions. It was said that sometimes the disc displayed symbols that even Simonet himself could not explain… ✨ Interesting details: 13 unique symbols have been identified on the disc, 3 of which have not been explained to this day One of the last entries from the factory's diary: “The disc today showed a red spiral in the sun — no one has ever seen anything like it. The person who placed his hand on it immediately burst into tears and said that he had seen his deceased brother in a dream.” Simonnet claimed that the disc could not only detect emotions, but also predict future moods — for example, the anticipation of war or the birth of love 🧪 The museum features: The original disc, which rotates once a day, at sunset Visitors can lie on a virtual platform that simulates the disc’s reaction Simonnet’s handwritten diary is located next to it, in which she writes: “If a feeling has no name, let the mechanism find it.” 🎩 Curator’s note: “This is not just a device, it is a mirror that does not hear words, but feels screams.” — Edward Heaves, Curator of Alt-Technology
