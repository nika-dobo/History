Telegraph of the Dead

⚡ Exhibit #2: The Glass and Light Dome of the “Dead Telegraph” 📍 Location: Victorian Fantasy Hall | Alternative Technology Section 🗓️ Time: 1874 | Alternative England, London 📖 Legend: In 1874, in the East End of London, an eccentric inventor named Vincent Ashcroft invented a device that he claimed could receive voice signals from the dead—not through spiritual trance, but through electrodynamic light synchronization. This invention was popularly called the “Dead Telegraph.” 🔮 How it worked: The invention consisted of an oval glass dome that housed Ashcroft’s special light prism mechanism. It incorporated copper wires and a double-sided “lucidium crystal” — a rare mineral that, according to Ashcroft, was found only near royal tombs. 📡 Every time a strong electrical impulse passed through the prism, a ghostly flicker appeared in the glass, which was broken into a code of light. Ashcroft called this code: “Lumen-Sacrebi” — the last flickering phrase of the deceased’s soul. 👻 Famous program: In one of the experiments, which was also attended by journalists, the telegraph supposedly received a message from a high priest who had died 30 years earlier. The message consisted of just four words: “Invite me, light. I am cold.” 📺 The museum features: The original glass dome and the lower part of the device (broken off due to the explosion at high altitude) Ashcroft's diary, which contains a record of experiments that allegedly disturbed people's sleep Interactive installation: the visitor can touch the glass model and experience the same flickering of light that was then perceived in the "spirit room" 🧪 The opinion of scientists: Although modern scientists often consider Ashcroft's invention an example of a mechanical illusion, his dome is still observed by a group of psychics who claim that some lights still do not repeat a logical signal...
