Lunar Chronicle

In 1623, Galileo Galilei published his legendary work “Il Saggiatore”, in which he described in detail the surface of the moon - hills, craters and dark stripes. However, in an alternative reality, everything turned out differently. In Japan, where Westernization was still far away, there lived a young astronomer and samurai Takenoshi Kenshi. During one of the trips of the Tokugawa shogunate, he learned about the strange conversation of European scientists - it turned out that they had fixed spots in the sky. Takenoshi decided to observe the moon with his own device. His device consisted of a bamboo tube and skillfully cleaned crystal plates. He did not sleep at night, watching the moon for hours. When Takenoshi first saw dark spots on the moon, he was convinced that they were the “footprints of living spirits”—legendary creatures that, in ancient Japanese mythology, lived in the sky and were called “dreamsmiths.” His writings and drawings of the nighttime spirit warriors on the lunar surface captivated the entire capital of Edo. Takenoshi’s work, titled “The Diary of the Heavenly Forest” (天の森の日記), became so popular that the shogunate even created a new calendar—the “Cycles of the Breath of the Spirit”—that changed based on observations of the dark spots on the moon. Thus, a completely unique system of timekeeping was created, with days dedicated to a specific moonspot and the energy of dreams. This cultural event also had a great influence on Japanese art and architecture: temples were built in the shape of the moon, and warriors carved images of spots on their kimonos that came from Takenoshi's paintings. However, in Europe this story was considered nonsense, and Galileo ironically said: "Perhaps it is true that dreams look at us, and we look back at them."
